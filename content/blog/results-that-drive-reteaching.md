A classroom activity result page should answer a practical teacher question:
what should I do with this information tomorrow?

Average score is useful, but it is not enough. A teacher needs to see which
items confused the class, which students need follow-up, and whether the
activity itself needs a better explanation.

## Item performance is the fastest reteach signal

When a class misses the same item, the next step is probably not more grading.
It is reteaching. That is why item-level correct rate matters.

A good result view should show:

- the item prompt
- the template type
- how many students submitted an answer
- the correct rate
- the expected answer
- the teacher's explanation when one exists

That turns results into a lesson-planning surface.

## Student summaries keep follow-up humane

Teachers also need a student-level view. One student may have one weak attempt.
Another may have three attempts and a clear pattern. The page should show latest
accuracy, average accuracy, best accuracy, number of items needing review, and
last submission time.

That summary helps teachers decide who needs a quick check-in, not just who got
the lowest score.

## Exports matter because classrooms have systems

Even if ClassGamify keeps results visible in the dashboard, teachers still need
to move evidence into gradebooks, parent notes, intervention logs, or offline
records. CSV export is not glamorous, but it is part of making the product fit
real school workflows.

The export should preserve summary data, attempt data, and item-level answers
so the teacher does not have to copy details by hand.

## The result page is part of the teaching loop

The assignment is not finished when the student submits. It is finished when the
teacher knows what to do next. That is why ClassGamify treats results as a core
workflow, not an afterthought.
